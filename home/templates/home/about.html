The Launch of the Open Visualization Academy

One professor’s mission to teach the world data visualization

By Lauren Comander

This article originally published by the University of Miami’s Frost Institute for Data Science and Computing (IDSC).

Back in 2012, when Massive Open Online Courses were all the rage, Alberto Cairo, Ph.D. sat at home with no budget, no staff, no fancy recording studio. It was just him, armed with a webcam and an idea.

“I collaborated with the Knight Center at the University of Texas and created a MOOC introducing the basics of data visualization, designing charts, graphs, maps, and so on,” he said. “It was just me at home recording lectures – but it became huge!”

Huge indeed. By 2019, tens of thousands of people from nearly 150 countries had enrolled in “Introduction to Infographics and Data Visualization,” the first journalism MOOC ever created.

So when he sat down last year to think about how to spend his budget as a Knight Chair in Visual Journalism, a position that tasks him with contributing to public knowledge and opening up access to the field, he asked himself a question: What project could have the greatest impact? Then he thought, what if I take the model of that MOOC and expand it?

By then, Cairo had spent years teaching and consulting internationally, often at the intersection of journalism, design, and emerging technology. Along the way, he’d helped shape how large audiences interpret complex data during a career that Microsoft described as “always at the vanguard of visual journalism.”

The seeds for the Open Visualization Academy had taken root. Now, with the first set of courses scheduled to launch January 31st, 2026, the vision that began as a sketch is finally becoming reality.

Building a public library of knowledge

The Academy is structured simply, with three main components.

The first is Cairo’s brand-new Introduction to Information Design and Data Visualization video course, where he spends six hours covering the fundamental principles of design and data visualization.

This time, though, Cairo knew he couldn’t be the only voice. With a network that extends beyond academia, he has worked with companies like Microsoft and Google, advised public-sector institutions like the Congressional Budget Office, and spoken at major conferences.

For the Academy’s second component, he used those industry connections to bring in subject-matter experts to teach specialized courses on topics ranging from cartography and mapping to coding and programming with R or Python.

“I essentially know everybody in the industry because of books I’ve written and talks I’ve given, or it’s two degrees of separation,” he said. “Think of it as a tree. My class is the root, and branches extend into specialized areas taught by experts. It feels like the culmination of decades of making and producing graphics, teaching, and organizing conferences. I’m bringing all of this together in a single place in collaboration with others.”

Another way Cairo has built shared infrastructure for the field is through publishing. He’s the author of four widely read books on visualization, with a fifth in the works.

The Academy’s third component is a MOOCthat Cairo will teach every year, with details still being worked out.

As the endowed Knight Chair in Visual Journalism and professor at the University of Miami’s School of Communication, Cairo’s role is not only to teach students at the University but also to contribute to the broader public understanding of his field. The Academy is built around the belief that anyone willing to put in the effort can learn this visual language.

“I see proof of that every semester!” he said. “Anyone willing to put in a bit of effort can improve how they create charts.”

Cairo plans to update the Academy regularly, pushing out new courses every year. “The project will keep evolving,” he said. He also maintains a weekly newsletter, produced with help from current and former students, that already counts more than 3,000 subscribers, and he’s using the newsletter and social media to spread the word about the Academy.

“My goal isn’t for it to explode immediately,” Cairo said. “I want to build a community that grows organically.”

Why good charts matter

The Academy isn’t training people to merely decorate stories with charts. In the era of viral charts and infographics, it’s training them to change how people think about information. The European Union tapped Cairo to help translate high-value public data into visual explanations meant to educate readers, and this idea of visualization as a public service is the same philosophy driving the Academy.

“Visualizations – charts, maps, graphs – are a language,” Cairo said. “They can inform or mislead. I want to help people create graphics that reveal the truth, not distort it.”

That idea sits at the heart of one of his books, How Charts Lie: Getting Smarter about Visual Information, which argues that the most dangerous graphics are the ones that feel persuasive while quietly bending the story.

Visualization, Cairo said, is also a gateway to other skills. “It’s an excellent way to teach introductory statistics or mathematical reasoning,” he said. “Designing a good graphic isn’t just about communicating with others. It helps you reason about information.”

From radio dreams to data visualization

A renowned visualization designer and former art director who has led graphics teams across several countries, Cairo joined the University of Miami in 2012 and later became the Knight Chair in Visual Journalism. He’s director of visualization at the University of Miami’s Frost Institute for Data Science and Computing. He continues to teach both undergraduate and graduate students while building tools and resources for a much wider public.

Cairo originally wanted to work in radio. The career pivot began when a professor who knew he could draw recommended an internship in a newspaper graphics department. He later began studying cartography on his own, which led him deeper into data visualization.

Hearing how his work helps people start careers or learn new skills, he said, is what makes life worth living as a professor.

“It’s knowing you helped someone learn something meaningful,” Cairo said. “To this day, when I travel, I meet people who tell me they began their careers in data visualization because they took one of my old MOOCs. It was free, open, and widely accessible. That’s what this Academy is really about.”
